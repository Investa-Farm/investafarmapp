Understood. You want a primary market pricing formula that is not simply dividing the farmer’s needed capital (10 × 1500). Instead, the share price should reflect the expected future revenue, risk, time to harvest, and the investor’s required return – just like a discounted cash flow model.

Below is a complete mathematical framework for primary market pricing, including how to determine the number of shares, share price, and investor revenue share (α) so that the farmer raises exactly K dollars.

---

2. Risk‑Adjusted Discount Factor

From the secondary market model, the risk‑adjusted discount factor that converts a future dollar into a present value (for investors) is:

d = \frac{1 - \lambda}{(1 + r_f)^{T/365}}

Where:

\lambda = \frac{10 - S}{9} \times p_{\max} \times L

· \lambda = proportional risk discount (0 … 1).
  Example: S=7, p_{\max}=0.4, L=0.8 → \lambda = (3/9)*0.4*0.8 = 0.1067.

Thus:

d = \frac{1 - \frac{10-S}{9} p_{\max} L}{(1 + r_f)^{T/365}}

---

3. Investor’s Total Present Value of Future Payout

At harvest, investors collectively receive \alpha \hat{R} dollars.
The present value of that amount (today’s worth) is:

\text{PV}_{\text{investors}} = \alpha \hat{R} \times d

This is the amount investors should be willing to pay today for the right to receive \alpha \hat{R} at harvest, given the risk and time.

---

4. Primary Market Consistency Condition

In the primary market, the total amount paid by investors today is exactly K (the farmer’s capital need). Therefore we must have:

K = \alpha \hat{R} \times d

This is the fundamental equation of primary pricing.
If this equation does not hold, the farmer cannot raise exactly K while offering a fixed \alpha – unless the platform adjusts \alpha or the share price deviates from fair value (which would create arbitrage).

---

5. Solving for Required \alpha or Maximum K

Case A – Platform fixes \alpha (e.g., 20% for this farm)
Then the maximum capital that can be raised at fair value is:

K_{\max} = \alpha \hat{R} d

If K_{\max} < K, the farmer must either:

· Reduce requested capital, or
· Increase \alpha (give investors a bigger revenue share), or
· The platform subsidises (not recommended).

Case B – Capital K is fixed (e.g., $15,000)
Then the required investor revenue share is:

\alpha_{\text{req}} = \frac{K}{\hat{R} d}

If \alpha_{\text{req}} falls outside [0.10, 0.30], the farm is not viable at that K and risk level.

Case C – Both K and \alpha are flexible
The platform can choose any pair that satisfies K = \alpha \hat{R} d. Then the farmer’s share \beta can be set independently (45–65%), and platform share \gamma = 1 - \alpha - \beta.

---

6. Determining N and P_0

Once K and \alpha are decided (via the above consistency), the platform is free to choose any integer number of shares N. The primary share price is then:

P_0 = \frac{K}{N}

Practical choice for N:

· Make P_0 a “nice” number for investors (e.g., $1, $5, $10).
· Or set N so that the minimum investment is low (e.g., $1 share → N = K shares).
· Or set N based on expected secondary market liquidity (too many shares = tiny price, too few = chunky).

Example:
K = 15,000, choose N = 1500 → P_0 = 10 (the original simple case).
But the key difference is: \alpha is not arbitrary – it must satisfy K = \alpha \hat{R} d. If that gives \alpha = 0.25, then the $10 share price is fair. If it gives \alpha = 0.12, then $10 is still fair – the pricing formula ensures no arbitrage.

7. Complete Algorithm for Replit (Primary Market)

Here’s the step‑by‑step calculation you can implement:

```python
# Primary market pricing for Investa Farm
# Given inputs
K = 15000                # dollars, farmer's capital need
R_hat = 100000           # forecast revenue (example)
T = 120                  # days to harvest
r_f = 0.10               # annual risk-free rate
S = 7                    # AI risk score (1-10)
p_max = 0.4
L = 0.8

# Calculate risk discount
loss_prob = ((10 - S) / 9.0) * p_max
risk_discount = loss_prob * L

# Time discount
time_discount = (1 + r_f) ** (T / 365.0)

# Combined discount factor d
d = (1 - risk_discount) / time_discount

# Required investor share alpha_req (if K is fixed)
alpha_req = K / (R_hat * d)

# Check viability
if 0.10 <= alpha_req <= 0.30:
    print(f"Viable: alpha = {alpha_req:.2%}")
else:
    print(f"Not viable: alpha = {alpha_req:.2%} (outside 10-30%)")

# Choose N (e.g., 1500 for nice $10 shares)
N = 1500
P0 = K / N

print(f"Primary share price: ${P0:.2f}")
print(f"Number of shares: {N}")
print(f"Investor revenue share required: {alpha_req:.2%}")
```
