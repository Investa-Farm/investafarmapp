🤔 Wait, Why Is This Called a "Stock Broker"?

The confusion makes sense because the term "stock broker" historically meant someone who executes trades. But in modern wealth management, full-service brokers do something very different—they build and manage portfolios for clients.

Here's the distinction:

Role What They Do Example
Execution‑only broker Takes your order and executes it Robinhood, E*TRADE
Full‑service broker / Portfolio manager Advises you, builds a portfolio, manages it Merrill Lynch wealth manager, robo‑advisors

What you're describing is the portfolio manager. They don't just execute—they construct and sell portfolios as investment products.

🔄 How Portfolio Management Actually Works

There are three main models, ordered from least to most automated:

1. Human Portfolio Manager (Full‑service model)

A real person at Investa Farm (or a partner firm) actively researches farms, constructs portfolios, and communicates with investors.

· Pro: High trust, personalised advice
· Con: Expensive, doesn't scale easily

2. Model Portfolios (Curated by experts, sold as products)

Investa Farm's in‑house team creates model portfolios with specific tickers or identifiers. Investors buy them as ready‑made products, but the team updates them periodically.

· Pro: Lower cost than full human management
· Con: Requires ongoing oversight

3. Algorithmic / Robo‑Advisor (Fully automated)

Investors fill out a questionnaire about their goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon. AI builds and rebalances a personalised portfolio, charging a small management fee (e.g., 0.25–0.50%).

· Pro: Scales infinitely, low cost
· Con: Requires sophisticated AI

In 2025, most platforms use a hybrid: robo‑advisors for the masses, human portfolio managers for high‑net‑worth clients【23†L1-L5】.

🧮 The Mathematics of Portfolio Construction

This is the genius part. Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT), developed by Harry Markowitz in 1952, explains why diversification works:

E(R_p) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i E(R_i)

Where:

· E(R_p) = Expected return of the portfolio
· w_i = Weight (percentage) of farm i in the portfolio
· E(R_i) = Expected return of farm i (from your AI yield forecast)

But the real magic is in the risk equation:

\sigma_p^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i^2 \sigma_i^2 + \sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{\substack{j=1 \\ j \neq i}}^{n} w_i w_j \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij}

This looks complex, but the core idea is simple: correlation is key. The total risk of a portfolio depends not just on each farm's individual risk (\sigma_i^2), but on how farms move together (\rho_{ij}).

· If Farm A (maize) and Farm B (beans) have low correlation (they don't fail at the same time), combining them reduces overall risk.
· If they have high correlation (both fail when drought hits), diversification doesn't help much.

This is why a good portfolio manager on your platform would combine different crops and regions.

🛡️ Regulatory Requirements for Portfolio Managers in Kenya

This is critical. Under Kenya's Capital Markets Authority (CMA), anyone managing investor portfolios must be licensed. Key requirements include:

Requirement Details
Licence type Fund Manager or Investment Adviser
Minimum capital KES 20,000,000 (~$155,000) for a Fund Manager
Key staff At least two approved key individuals (CIO, COO, Compliance Officer)
Custody Client assets must be held by an independent custodian
Reporting Quarterly and annual reports to CMA

Two practical paths:

1. Partner with an existing CMA‑licensed Fund Manager. They manage the portfolios; you provide the technology and distribution. This is the fastest, lowest‑risk path.
2. Become a licensed Fund Manager yourself. Takes 6–12 months and significant capital, but gives you full control.

For the MVP, partnering is strongly recommended.
