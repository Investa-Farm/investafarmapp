Here’s a mathematical model for secondary market pricing of farm shares on Investa Farm’s platform.

---

Core Assumptions

· A farm season has a total number of shares N, each originally sold at price P_0.
· The investor pool is entitled to a fixed percentage \alpha of harvest revenue (after farmer’s share).
  Example: \alpha = 0.50 if investors get 50% of revenue.
· At harvest, total revenue R is unknown but can be forecasted.
· Each share pays a dividend at harvest:
  D = \frac{\alpha R}{N}
· The current time is t (in days) from planting, with total season length T (days until harvest).
  Remaining time = T - t.

---

1. Fundamental value (expected payoff)

Let \hat{R}(t) be the AI‑based forecast of total harvest revenue at time t.
Then the expected dividend per share is:

\hat{D}(t) = \frac{\alpha \,\hat{R}(t)}{N}

If there were no risk and no time preference, a share’s fair price would be \hat{D}(t).
But investors discount for:

· Time value of money (opportunity cost)
· Risk (yield, price, weather, pest)
· Illiquidity (if secondary market is thin)

---

2. Discounted expected value

The classic pricing model for a future cash flow is:

P_{\text{fair}}(t) = \frac{\hat{D}(t)}{(1 + r)^{(T-t)/365}} \times (1 - \lambda)

Where:

· r = annual risk‑free rate (e.g., 10% for M‑Pesa savings? Or use a higher discount for agri risk)
· (T-t)/365 = remaining years
· \lambda = total risk discount (0 to 1), derived from AI risk score.

---

3. Risk discount from AI scoring

Let the farm’s AI risk score be S \in [1,10], where 10 = lowest risk, 1 = highest.
Define a loss probability p_{\text{loss}} based on historical data:

p_{\text{loss}} = \max\left(0, \min\left(1, \frac{10 - S}{9} \times p_{\max}\right)\right)

Where p_{\max} is the maximum loss probability when S=1 (e.g., 0.40 for 40% chance of significant crop failure).
Then the risk discount:

\lambda = p_{\text{loss}} \times L

Where L is the expected loss given default (e.g., 0.80 means you lose 80% of expected dividend if failure occurs).
Thus:

\lambda = \frac{10 - S}{9} \times p_{\max} \times L

Example: S = 7, p_{\max}=0.4, L=0.8 → \lambda = (3/9)\times 0.4\times 0.8 = 0.1067. So 10.7% discount.

---

4. Incorporating market dynamics (bid‑ask spread, liquidity premium)

In a secondary market, price also depends on supply/demand. Let:

· Q_{\text{buy}} = total buy orders (number of shares wanted)
· Q_{\text{sell}} = total sell orders

A simple clearing price P_{\text{market}} is:

P_{\text{market}} = P_{\text{fair}} \times \left(1 + \beta \cdot \frac{Q_{\text{buy}} - Q_{\text{sell}}}{Q_{\text{total}}} \right)

Where \beta is a sensitivity parameter (e.g., 0.2), and Q_{\text{total}} = total shares outstanding.
This pushes price above fair when demand exceeds supply, and below fair when supply exceeds demand.

Additionally, a liquidity discount \delta (e.g., 0.02 for 2%) is applied if the market is thin:

P_{\text{execution}} = P_{\text{market}} \times (1 - \delta)

This is the price a seller actually receives (or buyer pays, with an ask premium symmetric).

---

5. Complete pricing formula for a seller

For a seller wanting to list a share at time t:

\boxed{P_{\text{sell}} = \frac{\alpha \hat{R}(t)}{N(1 + r)^{(T-t)/365}} \times \left(1 - \frac{10 - S}{9} p_{\max} L \right) \times \left(1 + \beta \frac{Q_{\text{buy}} - Q_{\text{sell}}}{Q_{\text{total}}} \right) \times (1 - \delta)}

And the buyer’s price would be P_{\text{sell}} / (1 - \delta) if platform takes no spread, but typically a small trading fee is added.

---

6. Worked example

· \alpha = 0.50, N = 10{,}000 shares
· \hat{R}(t) = 2{,}000{,}000 KES forecast revenue
· T-t = 60 days, r = 0.10 annual → (1+r)^{60/365} = 1.10^{0.1644} \approx 1.0158
· S = 7, p_{\max}=0.4, L=0.8 → \lambda = (3/9)\times0.4\times0.8 = 0.1067
· Q_{\text{buy}} = 2{,}000, Q_{\text{sell}} = 1{,}000, Q_{\text{total}}=10{,}000 → imbalance = (2000-1000)/10000 = 0.10
· \beta = 0.2 → multiplier = 1 + 0.2\times0.10 = 1.02
· \delta = 0.02 (2% liquidity discount)

First, expected dividend = (0.5 \times 2M)/10k = 100 KES.
Discounted = 100 / 1.0158 \approx 98.44 KES.
Risk discount = 98.44 \times (1 - 0.1067) = 87.94 KES.
Demand/supply adjustment = 87.94 \times 1.02 = 89.70 KES.
Liquidity discount = 89.70 \times 0.98 = 87.91 KES.

Final seller price ≈ 87.91 KES (original P_0 might have been 50 KES, so this is a premium).

---

7. Practical implementation notes

· The AI model updates \hat{R}(t) and S daily – price changes accordingly.
· The platform can show a fair value range (bid/ask) instead of a single price.
· To prevent manipulation, order book imbalances are smoothed over 1 hour.
· For extreme situations (e.g., drought), p_{\max} may increase dynamically.

This model gives investors confidence that prices reflect real information, while still allowing emotional trading (fear, greed) via the demand/supply term.
